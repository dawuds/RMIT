## 1. Executive Summary

*Provide a high-level overview of this framework's purpose, its strategic importance to the organization, and the key governance principles it establishes. This section should be suitable for senior management and board-level readers and should not exceed two pages.*

[Organization Name] recognizes that effective governance of technology projects is a critical enabler of business strategy and a fundamental requirement for regulatory compliance. This IT Project Governance Framework ("the Framework") establishes the structures, processes, standards, and accountabilities that govern the planning, execution, and delivery of all information technology projects across [Organization Name].

This Framework has been developed in alignment with Bank Negara Malaysia's Risk Management in Technology (RMiT) Policy Document, specifically Clause 10.1, which mandates that financial institutions establish robust governance arrangements for technology projects. The Framework supports [Organization Name]'s commitment to managing technology risk prudently and delivering IT investments that generate measurable business value while maintaining the highest standards of security, resilience, and regulatory compliance.

### 1.1 Strategic Imperative

*Describe why IT project governance is strategically important to the organization, linking it to business strategy, regulatory obligations, and risk appetite.*

| Strategic Driver | Description |
|---|---|
| **Regulatory Compliance** | Adherence to BNM RMiT Clause 10.1 and related regulatory obligations |
| **Risk Management** | Proactive identification and management of technology project risks |
| **Value Delivery** | Ensuring IT investments deliver intended business outcomes |
| **Resource Optimization** | Efficient allocation of human, financial, and technology resources |
| **Stakeholder Confidence** | Maintaining board and senior management confidence in IT delivery capability |

### 1.2 Key Governance Principles

*List the foundational principles that underpin this framework. These should reflect the organization's values and risk culture.*

The following principles guide all IT project governance activities at [Organization Name]:

1. **Accountability** — Every project has a clearly defined accountable owner at an appropriate level of seniority.
2. **Transparency** — Project status, risks, issues, and financial performance are reported accurately and in a timely manner.
3. **Proportionality** — Governance intensity is proportionate to project complexity, cost, risk, and strategic significance.
4. **Compliance by Design** — Regulatory and policy requirements are embedded into project processes from inception.
5. **Continuous Improvement** — Lessons learned are systematically captured and applied to improve future project delivery.
6. **Stakeholder Engagement** — Business and technology stakeholders are actively engaged throughout the project lifecycle.
7. **Ethical Use of Technology** — Projects involving data, artificial intelligence, or customer-facing systems adhere to ethical standards and data protection requirements.

## 2. Purpose and Scope

*Define clearly what this document is intended to achieve and the boundaries of its applicability. Precision in scope definition is important for audit purposes.*

### 2.1 Purpose

This IT Project Governance Framework establishes the mandatory governance requirements for all technology projects undertaken by or on behalf of [Organization Name]. It defines the governance structures, processes, roles, responsibilities, decision rights, and controls that ensure IT projects are initiated, planned, executed, monitored, and closed in a manner consistent with [Organization Name]'s strategic objectives, risk appetite, and regulatory obligations.

Specifically, this Framework is designed to:

- Provide a consistent, structured approach to IT project governance across [Organization Name] and its subsidiaries.
- Ensure compliance with Bank Negara Malaysia's RMiT Policy Document, Clause 10.1, and all applicable regulatory requirements.
- Enable effective oversight of the IT project portfolio by senior management and the Board.
- Establish clear decision-making authorities and escalation pathways.
- Define minimum standards for project documentation, reporting, and quality assurance.
- Facilitate effective management of technology risk throughout the project lifecycle.
- Support post-implementation accountability and benefits realization.

#### 2.2.2 Projects in Scope

*Define the types and characteristics of projects that fall within this framework.*

This Framework applies to all IT projects that meet one or more of the following criteria:

- Projects involving development, implementation, enhancement, or decommissioning of technology systems, platforms, or infrastructure.
- Projects with a total budget exceeding **RM [Threshold Amount]**.
- Projects with a duration exceeding **[X] calendar months**.

- Routine maintenance activities that do not alter system functionality.
- Emergency changes required to address critical security vulnerabilities (subject to post-implementation review).

> **Note:** Exclusion from full governance does not exempt a project from change management controls, security review, or regulatory notification requirements where applicable.

## 5. IT Project Lifecycle Methodology

*Describe the standard project lifecycle adopted by the organization, explaining each phase and its key activities, inputs, and outputs.*

### 5.1 Overview

[Organization Name] adopts a structured five-phase project lifecycle for all IT projects in scope of this Framework. The lifecycle is aligned with industry standards including PMBOK and PRINCE2, and adapted to meet the specific requirements of a regulated financial institution.

| Phase | Name | Key Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Phase 1 | **Initiation** | Define the project concept, confirm strategic alignment, and obtain approval to proceed |
| Phase 2 | **Planning** | Develop detailed plans, secure resources, and obtain formal approval to execute |
| Phase 3 | **Execution** | Build, test, and implement the solution |
| Phase 4 | **Monitoring & Control** | Track progress, manage risks and changes, and maintain governance oversight |
| Phase 5 | **Closure** | Formally close the project, capture lessons learned, and initiate benefits tracking |

> **Note:** Phases 3 and 4 operate concurrently throughout the execution period.

### 5.2 Phase 1: Initiation

*Describe the activities, inputs, outputs, and responsibilities for the Initiation phase.*

**Purpose:** To establish the project concept, confirm strategic alignment, assess high-level feasibility, and obtain authorization to proceed to detailed planning.

**Key Activities:**

- Develop the Project Concept Note / Project Charter.
- Conduct high-level business case assessment.
- Identify key stakeholders.
- Perform preliminary risk and regulatory impact assessment.
- Determine project tier and governance requirements.
- Obtain Gate 1 approval.

**Inputs:**

- Business strategy and IT roadmap.
- Regulatory change requirements.
- Business problem statement or opportunity.
- High-level budget estimate.

**Outputs / Deliverables:**

| Deliverable | Responsible | Mandatory? | Template Reference |
|---|---|---|---|
| Project Concept Note | Business Owner / Project Sponsor | Yes | PMO-TMPL-001 |
| Project Charter | PMO / Project Manager | Yes | PMO-TMPL-002 |
| Preliminary Risk Assessment | Risk Management | Yes | PMO-TMPL-003 |
| Regulatory Impact Assessment | Compliance | Yes | PMO-TMPL-004 |
| Stakeholder Register | Project Manager | Yes | PMO-TMPL-005 |
| Gate 1 Decision Record | PMO | Yes | PMO-TMPL-006 |

**Gate 1 — Initiation Approval:**

- **Decision:** Proceed to Planning / Defer / Reject.
- **Decision Authority:** PMO Director (Tier 3), PGB (Tier 2), ITSC (Tier 1).
- **Criteria:** Strategic alignment confirmed; high-level business case viable; regulatory impact assessed; resource availability indicated.

### 5.3 Phase 2: Planning

**Purpose:** To develop comprehensive project plans covering scope, schedule, budget, resources, risks, quality, and communications; and to obtain formal approval to proceed to execution.

**Key Activities:**

- Develop detailed project plan and work breakdown structure (WBS).
- Prepare detailed business case and financial model.
- Define project scope and requirements.
- Develop risk management plan and initial risk register.
- Develop change management plan.
- Prepare procurement plan for vendor engagements.
- Conduct security and privacy impact assessment.
- Define quality assurance approach and test strategy.
- Obtain Gate 2 approval.

**Outputs / Deliverables:**

| Deliverable | Responsible | Mandatory? | Template Reference |
|---|---|---|---|
| Detailed Project Plan | Project Manager | Yes | PMO-TMPL-007 |
| Work Breakdown Structure | Project Manager | Yes | PMO-TMPL-008 |
| Detailed Business Case | Project Sponsor / Finance | Yes | PMO-TMPL-009 |
| Risk Register | Project Manager / Risk | Yes | PMO-TMPL-010 |
| Change Management Plan | Project Manager | Yes | PMO-TMPL-011 |
| Procurement Plan | Project Manager / Procurement | Conditional | PMO-TMPL-012 |
| Security Impact Assessment | Information Security | Yes | PMO-TMPL-013 |
| Privacy Impact Assessment | Data Protection Officer | Conditional | PMO-TMPL-014 |
| Test Strategy | QA Lead | Yes | PMO-TMPL-015 |
| Communication Plan | Project Manager | Yes | PMO-TMPL-016 |
| Gate 2 Decision Record | PMO | Yes | PMO-TMPL-006 |

**Gate 2 — Planning Approval (Project Authorization):**

- **Decision:** Proceed to Execution / Return for Revision / Cancel.
- **Decision Authority:** PGB (Tier 3), ITSC (Tier 2), ITSC + MRC (Tier 1).
- **Criteria:** Detailed plan approved; budget authorized; resources confirmed; risks assessed and mitigated; regulatory requirements confirmed; procurement strategy approved.

### 5.4 Phase 3: Execution

**Purpose:** To build, configure, test, and deploy the agreed solution in accordance with the approved project plan.

**Key Sub-Phases:**

| Sub-Phase | Description |
|---|---|
| **Design** | Technical design and architecture definition |
| **Build / Configure** | Development, configuration, and integration activities |
| **System Testing** | Unit, integration, and system testing |
| **User Acceptance Testing (UAT)** | Business validation of the solution |
| **Deployment Planning** | Cutover planning, training, and operational readiness |
| **Go-Live** | Production deployment |

**Key Activities:**

- Execute project plan in accordance with approved methodology.
- Manage vendor and contractor performance.
- Maintain and update project documentation.
- Conduct regular status reporting.
- Manage changes through formal change control.
- Execute testing strategy and document results.
- Prepare go-live readiness assessment.
- Obtain Gate 3 approval (Go-Live Authorization).

**Gate 3 — Go-Live Authorization:**

- **Decision:** Approve Go-Live / Defer Go-Live / Cancel.
- **Decision Authority:** PGB (Tier 3), Project Sponsor + ITSC Chair (Tier 2), ITSC (Tier 1).
- **Criteria:** All testing phases completed with approved results; go-live readiness checklist signed off; rollback plan approved; operational readiness confirmed; regulatory notifications submitted (where required).

### 5.5 Phase 4: Monitoring and Control

*This phase runs concurrently with Phases 2, 3, and the first period of operations post-go-live.*

**Key Activities:**

- Track project progress against plan (schedule, budget, scope).
- Manage and escalate risks and issues.
- Process change requests through formal change control.
- Report project status to PGB, PMO, and ITSC.
- Monitor vendor performance.
- Manage stakeholder communications.
- Conduct periodic health checks and assurance reviews.

### 5.6 Phase 5: Closure

**Purpose:** To formally close the project, document lessons learned, transition to operations, and initiate benefits realization tracking.

**Key Activities:**

- Confirm all deliverables have been accepted by the business.
- Complete all project documentation and archive.
- Conduct lessons learned workshop.
- Prepare project closure report.
- Formally release project resources.
- Hand over to operations / business as usual (BAU).
- Initiate post-implementation review (PIR) process.
- Obtain Gate 4 approval (Project Closure).

**Outputs / Deliverables:**

| Deliverable | Responsible | Mandatory? | Template Reference |
|---|---|---|---|
| Project Closure Report | Project Manager | Yes | PMO-TMPL-017 |
| Lessons Learned Register | Project Manager | Yes | PMO-TMPL-018 |
| Benefits Realization Plan | Project Sponsor | Yes | PMO-TMPL-019 |
| Operational Handover Document | Project Manager / IT Ops | Yes | PMO-TMPL-020 |
| Gate 4 Decision Record (Closure) | PMO | Yes | PMO-TMPL-006 |

**Gate 4 — Project Closure Approval:**

- **Decision:** Approve Closure / Require Outstanding Actions.
- **Decision Authority:** PMO Director (Tier 3), PGB (Tier 2), ITSC (Tier 1).
- **Criteria:** All deliverables accepted; documentation complete; operational handover complete; lessons learned captured; benefits realization plan activated.

## 6. Project Categorization and Classification

*Define the criteria used to classify projects into tiers, and explain how classification determines governance requirements.*

### 6.1 Purpose of Project Classification

Project classification ensures that governance effort and oversight intensity are proportionate to the risk, complexity, and strategic significance of each project. All projects in scope of this Framework shall be classified at initiation and the classification reviewed at each gate.

### 6.2 Project Tier Classification

| Tier | Classification | Budget Threshold | Duration | Risk Rating | Strategic Impact | Governance Level |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Tier 1** | Strategic / High-Impact | > RM [Amount] | > [X] months | High / Critical | Enterprise-wide | ITSC + Board oversight |
| **Tier 2** | Significant | RM [Amount] – RM [Amount] | [X] – [X] months | Medium / High | Business unit-wide | ITSC oversight |
| **Tier 3** | Standard | < RM [Amount] | < [X] months | Low / Medium | Departmental | PGB oversight |

> **Note:** Any project may be escalated to a higher tier at the discretion of the PMO Director or Risk Management based on risk or strategic significance, regardless of budget or duration.

### 6.3 Risk-Based Classification Criteria

*In addition to tier classification, all projects shall be assessed against the following risk dimensions.*

| Risk Dimension | Rating Criteria | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| **Regulatory Impact** | Degree to which project affects compliance with regulatory requirements | High |
| **Data Sensitivity** | Whether the project involves personal, confidential, or regulated data | High |
| **Systems Criticality** | Whether the project affects core banking, payments, or other critical systems | High |
| **Third-Party Dependencies** | Extent of reliance on external vendors or outsourced services | Medium |
| **Technical Complexity** | Novelty of technology, integration complexity, and architectural impact | Medium |
| **Business Impact** | Operational disruption risk during implementation | Medium |
| **Resource Concentration** | Dependence on key individuals or scarce skills | Low |

### 6.4 Classification Process

1. Project Sponsor completes the **Project Classification Worksheet** (PMO-TMPL-021) at initiation.
2. PMO reviews the classification and confirms or adjusts.
3. Risk Management reviews classification for projects with a preliminary risk rating of Medium or above.
4. Final classification is recorded in the Project Charter and Gate 1 Decision Record.
5. Classification is reviewed at each gate and may be revised if project characteristics change materially.

## 7. Approval Gates and Decision Points

*Describe the stage-gate model in detail, including the criteria, documentation requirements, and decision authorities for each gate.*

### 7.1 Overview of the Stage-Gate Model

[Organization Name] employs a mandatory stage-gate model for all IT projects. Progress from one phase to the next requires explicit approval at a defined gate. No project may proceed to the next phase without gate approval, except in emergency circumstances as defined in Section 7.6.

| Gate | Name | Phase Transition | Primary Decision Authority |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Gate 0** | Portfolio Entry | Pre-Initiation → Initiation | PMO Director / ITSC |
| **Gate 1** | Initiation Approval | Initiation → Planning | PMO Director / PGB / ITSC |
| **Gate 2** | Project Authorization | Planning → Execution | PGB / ITSC / ITSC + MRC |
| **Gate 3** | Go-Live Authorization | Execution → Operations | PGB / Project Sponsor / ITSC |
| **Gate 4** | Project Closure | Operations → Closed | PMO Director / PGB / ITSC |

### 7.2 Gate 0 — Portfolio Entry

| Attribute | Details |
|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Confirm that a proposed project should enter the project portfolio and proceed to initiation |

## 11. Risk Management in IT Projects

*Define the approach to risk management throughout the IT project lifecycle, aligned with the enterprise risk management framework.*

### 11.1 Principles of Project Risk Management

IT project risk management at [Organization Name] is governed by the following principles:

- Risk management is a continuous activity throughout the project lifecycle, not a one-time exercise.
- All material risks must be identified, assessed, and actively managed.
- Risk management is integrated with the enterprise risk management framework.
- Risk owners are assigned for all risks; ownership cannot be delegated to the Project Manager alone.
- Risk appetite for IT projects is set by the CRO in alignment with the enterprise risk appetite statement.

### 11.2 Risk Identification

*All projects shall maintain a Risk Register using the PMO standard template (PMO-TMPL-010). Risk identification shall be conducted:*

- At project initiation (preliminary risk assessment).
- At the commencement of the planning phase (detailed risk assessment workshop).
- At each project gate review.
- At the commencement of each new project phase.
- On an ongoing basis throughout the project (continuous risk identification).
- Following any material change to project scope, schedule, or budget.

**Risk Categories for IT Projects:**

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Technical Risk** | Technology immaturity, integration complexity, architectural risk, technical debt |
| **Resource Risk** | Key person dependency, skill gaps, resource availability |
| **Vendor / Third-Party Risk** | Vendor capability, contract terms, geopolitical risk, vendor financial stability |
| **Regulatory / Compliance Risk** | Regulatory change, non-compliance, BNM notification requirements |
| **Security Risk** | Cybersecurity vulnerabilities, data breach, unauthorized access |
| **Data Risk** | Data quality, data migration, privacy risk |
| **Business Risk** | Business change resistance, benefit realization risk, business continuity impact |
| **Financial Risk** | Budget overrun, exchange rate (for offshore projects), cost estimation accuracy |
| **Schedule Risk** | Delays in dependencies, unrealistic timelines, scope creep |

### 11.3 Risk Assessment

All identified risks shall be assessed using [Organization Name]'s standard risk assessment methodology:

**Risk Likelihood Scale:**

| Rating | Description | Probability |
|---|---|---|
| 1 — Rare | May occur only in exceptional circumstances | < 10% |
| 2 — Unlikely | Could occur at some time | 10% – 29% |
| 3 — Possible | Might occur at some time | 30% – 49% |
| 4 — Likely | Will probably occur in most circumstances | 50% – 74% |
| 5 — Almost Certain | Expected to occur in most circumstances | ≥ 75% |

**Risk Impact Scale:**

| Rating | Description | Impact on Project |
|---|---|---|
| 1 — Negligible | Minimal impact; easily absorbed | < [X]% budget / schedule variance |
| 2 — Minor | Small impact; manageable within project | [X]% – [X]% budget / schedule variance |
| 3 — Moderate | Moderate impact; requires management attention | [X]% – [X]% budget / schedule variance |
| 4 — Significant | Significant impact; escalation to PGB required | [X]% – [X]% budget / schedule variance |
| 5 — Critical | Major impact; project viability at risk | > [X]% budget / schedule variance |

**Risk Rating Matrix:**

| | **Impact 1** | **Impact 2** | **Impact 3** | **Impact 4** | **Impact 5** |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Likelihood 5** | Medium (5) | High (10) | High (15) | Critical (20) | Critical (25) |
| **Likelihood 4** | Low (4) | Medium (8) | High (12) | High (16) | Critical (20) |
| **Likelihood 3** | Low (3) | Medium (6) | Medium (9) | High (12) | High (15) |
| **Likelihood 2** | Low (2) | Low (4) | Medium (6) | Medium (8) | High (10) |
| **Likelihood 1** | Low (1) | Low (2) | Low (3) | Low (4) | Medium (5) |

### 11.4 Risk Response Strategies

| Strategy | Description | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Avoid** | Eliminate the risk by changing the project plan | Risk rating is Critical and cannot be reduced |
| **Mitigate** | Reduce the likelihood or impact of the risk | Most risks; standard response for High and above |
| **Transfer** | Transfer the risk to a third party (e.g., insurance, contractual) | Vendor-related risks; financial risks |
| **Accept** | Acknowledge the risk and monitor | Low risks within risk appetite |
| **Escalate** | Escalate risks beyond the project's authority to manage | Risks with enterprise-level impact |

## 12. Change Management and Scope Control

*Define the processes for managing changes to project scope, schedule, and budget.*

### 12.1 Change Control Principles

Effective change control is essential to maintaining project integrity and governance accountability. At [Organization Name]:

- All changes to approved project scope, schedule, or budget must be formally documented and approved before implementation.
- Changes that exceed defined thresholds require escalated approval.
- Scope creep — the gradual expansion of project scope without formal approval — is not permitted.
- All approved changes are reflected in updated project plans and documented in the Change Register.

### 12.2 Change Classification

| Change Type | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Type 1 — Minor** | Change with no material impact on scope, schedule, or budget | Minor task resequencing; tool substitution with equivalent capability |
| **Type 2 — Moderate** | Change with moderate impact within project contingency | Schedule slip < [X] weeks; budget overrun within contingency; non-material scope adjustment |
| **Type 3 — Significant** | Change with material impact on scope, schedule, or approved budget | New functionality added; schedule slip > [X] weeks; cost increase > [X]% of approved budget |
| **Type 4 — Critical** | Change that fundamentally alters project objectives, benefits, or viability | Major scope reduction; budget increase > [X]%; change of strategic direction |

### 12.3 Change Approval Authority

| Change Type | Approval Authority |
|---|---|
| **Type 1 — Minor** | Project Manager |
| **Type 2 — Moderate** | Project Sponsor |
| **Type 3 — Significant** | PGB (Tier 3), ITSC (Tier 1 and 2) |
| **Type 4 — Critical** | ITSC + Business Case revision required |

### 12.4 Change Request Process

1. Requestor submits Change Request Form (PMO-TMPL-022) to Project Manager.
2. Project Manager assesses impact on scope, schedule, budget, risk, and regulatory compliance.
3. Project Manager classifies the change and routes for approval.
4. Approved changes are recorded in the Change Register (PMO-TMPL-023).
5. Project plans and documentation are updated to reflect approved changes.
6. Change status is reported in the next project status report.

## 14. Quality Assurance and Testing Standards

*Define the quality assurance approach and testing standards for IT projects.*

### 14.1 Quality Management Principles

Quality management in IT projects at [Organization Name] is founded on the principle that quality must be planned and built into the project from inception, not inspected in at the end. All projects shall:

- Define quality objectives and success criteria at initiation.
- Develop a Quality Management Plan as part of the project planning phase.
- Conduct quality reviews at each project gate.
- Maintain traceability between requirements, design, and test cases.
- Document and track all defects through to resolution.

### 14.2 Testing Phases

| Testing Phase | Description | Mandatory? | Responsibility |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Unit Testing** | Testing of individual components or modules | Yes (for development projects) | Development Lead |
| **System Integration Testing (SIT)** | Testing of integrated system components | Yes | QA Team |
| **User Acceptance Testing (UAT)** | Business validation of the solution against requirements | Yes | Business Owner / Project Sponsor |
| **Performance / Load Testing** | Validation of system performance under expected load | Yes (for Tier 1/2) | QA Team / IT Ops |
| **Security Testing / VAPT** | Vulnerability assessment and penetration testing | Yes (for internet-facing or critical systems) | CISO / Approved Vendor |
| **Regression Testing** | Ensuring existing functionality is not impacted | Yes | QA Team |
| **Disaster Recovery Testing** | Validation of DR procedures for critical systems | Yes (for critical systems) | IT Ops |
| **Parallel Running** | Running new and old systems simultaneously for validation | As required | Project Manager / IT Ops |

### 14.3 UAT Completion Criteria

- [ ] All in-scope test scenarios have been executed.
- [ ] Zero open Critical or High severity defects.
- [ ] All open Medium severity defects have an accepted workaround or approved resolution plan.
- [ ] Business Owner has provided formal sign-off on UAT completion.
- [ ] UAT completion report has been submitted to the PMO.

### 14.4 Defect Management

| Severity | Description | Resolution Target |

| **High** | Major functionality impaired; significant business impact | Resolved before go-live |

## 17. Post-Implementation Review

*Define the requirements for conducting post-implementation reviews (PIR) of IT projects.*

### 17.1 Purpose and Scope of PIR

The Post-Implementation Review (PIR) is a mandatory governance activity for all Tier 1 and Tier 2 projects, and is recommended for Tier 3 projects. The PIR evaluates:

- Whether the project delivered the agreed objectives and scope.
- Actual versus planned cost and schedule performance.
- Early indication of benefits realization against the business case.
- Effectiveness of project governance and management processes.
- Lessons learned for application to future projects.
- Outstanding issues or risks to be monitored post-closure.

### 17.2 PIR Timing

| Project Tier | PIR Timing | Mandatory? |
|---|---|---|
| **Tier 1** | [X] months after go-live (initial); [X] months after go-live (final) | Yes |
| **Tier 2** | [X] months after go-live | Yes |
| **Tier 3** | [X] months after go-live | Recommended |

### 17.3 PIR Process

1. PMO schedules PIR at project closure and notifies Project Sponsor and Business Owner.
2. Project Manager prepares PIR Report (PMO-TMPL-025) in advance of the PIR meeting.
3. PIR meeting is chaired by the Project Sponsor and attended by key stakeholders.
4. PIR report is finalized and submitted to PGB.
5. Lessons learned are recorded in the organizational knowledge base.
6. Any outstanding actions are assigned to owners with target completion dates.
7. PIR completion is recorded by PMO and included in portfolio reporting.

### 17.4 PIR Report Structure

| Section | Content |
|---|---|
| **Executive Summary** | Overview of project, key findings, and recommendations |
| **Project Overview** | Original objectives, scope, and approved parameters |
| **Delivery Performance** | Actual vs. planned scope, schedule, and budget |
| **Benefits Realization** | Early benefits realization assessment against business case |
| **Risk and Issue Review** | Retrospective review of how risks and issues were managed |
| **Governance Assessment** | Assessment of project governance effectiveness |
| **Stakeholder Satisfaction** | Feedback from key stakeholders |
| **Lessons Learned** | Key lessons for future projects (positive and negative) |
| **Recommendations** | Actions recommended for PMO, IT, and business stakeholders |
| **Outstanding Actions** | Actions arising from the PIR with owners and due dates |

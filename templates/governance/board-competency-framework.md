## 1. Executive Summary

*Provide a concise overview of the Board Competency Framework, its strategic rationale, alignment with regulatory expectations, and intended outcomes. This section should be suitable for presentation to senior stakeholders and regulators without requiring reference to the full document.*

### 1.1 Overview

[Organization Name] is committed to maintaining a Board of Directors with the requisite knowledge, skills, and experience to provide effective oversight of technology risk and digital strategy in an increasingly complex and regulated environment. This Board Competency Framework ("the Framework") establishes the structured approach by which [Organization Name] identifies, assesses, develops, and monitors the technology-related competencies of its Board members.

This Framework has been developed in direct response to the expectations articulated in Bank Negara Malaysia's ("BNM") Policy Document on Risk Management in Technology ("RMiT"), specifically Clause 8.3, which mandates that financial institutions ensure their Board and senior management possess adequate technology competencies to fulfil their governance and oversight responsibilities.

### 1.2 Strategic Context

*Describe the broader strategic environment driving the need for this Framework, including the institution's digital transformation agenda, the evolving cyber threat landscape, and regulatory expectations.*

[Organization Name] operates within a rapidly evolving digital financial services ecosystem. The following strategic drivers necessitate a structured approach to Board technology competency:

- **Regulatory Obligations:** BNM RMiT Clause 8.3 explicitly requires financial institutions to ensure Board members have adequate understanding of technology risks and controls.
- **Digital Transformation:** [Organization Name]'s [Digital Strategy / Transformation Programme Name] requires Board-level technology literacy to provide informed governance oversight.
- **Cybersecurity Threat Landscape:** The increasing sophistication of cyber threats demands that Board members understand the institution's risk posture and the adequacy of mitigation measures.
- **Third-Party and Cloud Risk:** Growing reliance on technology service providers and cloud infrastructure necessitates Board-level awareness of associated risks.
- **Data Governance:** The protection of customer data and compliance with the Personal Data Protection Act 2010 ("PDPA") requires technology-informed governance.

## 4. Methodology and Approach

*Describe the principles, design rationale, and methodological foundations underpinning this Framework, including how competency domains and proficiency levels were determined.*

### 4.1 Framework Design Principles

This Framework has been designed in accordance with the following principles:

| Principle | Description |
|---|---|
| **Proportionality** | Competency requirements are proportionate to each Board member's role, responsibilities, and committee membership. Not all Board members are expected to possess the same depth of technical knowledge. |
| **Collective Adequacy** | While individual minimum standards apply, the Framework emphasizes the adequacy of the Board's *collective* technology competency, recognizing that the Board functions as a governance body rather than as individual practitioners. |
| **Outcome-Orientation** | Competency requirements are defined in terms of governance outcomes (i.e., what a Board member needs to understand and do) rather than in terms of technical qualifications or credentials. |
| **Regulatory Alignment** | The Framework directly addresses the expectations of BNM RMiT Clause 8.3 and is consistent with BNM's Corporate Governance Policy and relevant supervisory expectations. |
| **Continuous Improvement** | The Framework is designed to evolve in response to changes in technology risk, the regulatory environment, and [Organization Name]'s strategic direction. |
| **Objectivity** | Competency assessments are conducted using structured, consistent tools to minimize subjectivity and enable meaningful comparison over time. |
| **Confidentiality** | Individual competency assessment results are treated as confidential personal information and handled in accordance with [Organization Name]'s Privacy Policy and the PDPA. |

### 4.2 Development Methodology

*Describe the process by which this Framework was developed, including stakeholder consultation, benchmarking, and regulatory engagement.*

This Framework was developed through the following process:

**Phase 1: Regulatory and Environmental Analysis**

- Review of BNM RMiT Policy Document, with specific focus on Clause 8.3 and related governance provisions
- Review of BNM Corporate Governance Policy
- Review of industry guidance on Board technology competency from [ISACA / IIF / FSB / Other Relevant Bodies]
- Analysis of [Organization Name]'s technology risk profile and strategic technology initiatives
- Benchmarking against peer financial institutions' Board competency frameworks [where available and appropriate]

**Phase 2: Competency Domain Design**

- Identification of technology competency domains relevant to Board-level governance oversight
- Consultation with [Group CIO / CTO / CISO / CRO] on domain currency and relevance
- Consultation with BNRC members on governance utility and practicality
- Review by external [technology governance / regulatory] advisors [if applicable]

**Phase 3: Proficiency Level Design**

- Definition of a four-tier proficiency scale calibrated to governance oversight requirements
- Alignment of proficiency levels with the principle of proportionality
- Testing of proficiency descriptors against sample Board member scenarios

**Phase 4: Assessment Tool Development**

- Development of structured self-assessment questionnaire
- Development of facilitator-led assessment methodology
- Piloting with [select Board members / senior management] and refinement

**Phase 5: Consultation and Approval**

- Submission to BNRC for review and comment
- Submission to full Board for approval
- Notation with BNM [if required / if proactively shared]

### 4.3 Competency Domain Selection Rationale

*Explain why the seven competency domains were selected as most relevant to Board-level technology governance oversight at [Organization Name].*

The seven technology competency domains defined in Section 5 were selected based on the following criteria:

1. **Regulatory Relevance:** Direct alignment with technology governance topics explicitly referenced in BNM RMiT and related BNM policy documents.
2. **Risk Materiality:** Relevance to the material technology risks identified in [Organization Name]'s Technology Risk Register and Risk Appetite Statement.
3. **Strategic Significance:** Connection to [Organization Name]'s technology strategy, digital transformation programme, and key technology investments.
4. **Board Actionability:** The domain represents an area where Board-level understanding translates directly into more effective governance oversight, challenge, and decision-making.
5. **Regulatory Developments:** Alignment with emerging regulatory focus areas, including cloud computing, artificial intelligence, and operational resilience.

### 4.4 Assessment Methodology Overview

*Summarize the assessment approach. Full details appear in Section 9.*

Board technology competency is assessed through a combination of the following methods:

| Method | Description | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| **Structured Self-Assessment** | Board members complete a structured questionnaire rating their own proficiency across the seven competency domains | Annual |
| **Facilitator-Led Assessment** | A structured discussion facilitated by [the CIO/CISO/external assessor] to validate self-assessment results and explore competency depth | Annual / Biennial |
| **Continuous Observation** | Qualitative observation of Board members' engagement with technology topics in Board and committee meetings, documented by the Company Secretary | Ongoing |
| **Development Activity Record** | Tracking of completed training, briefings, and other development activities as evidence of competency maintenance | Ongoing |
| **360-Degree Input (Optional)** | Input from management presenters and relevant executives on their assessment of individual Board member engagement and understanding | [Annual / As required] |

### 4.5 Competency Maintenance Philosophy

*Describe the institution's philosophy regarding ongoing competency maintenance, distinguishing between one-time attainment and continuous currency.*

[Organization Name] recognizes that technology is a rapidly evolving field and that achieving a competency threshold at a point in time does not guarantee ongoing competency. Accordingly, this Framework adopts a **continuous competency maintenance** philosophy, which holds that:

- Board members must actively maintain their technology knowledge currency through regular development activities.
- The relevance and adequacy of existing competency levels must be reassessed in light of material changes to [Organization Name]'s technology environment and the external threat landscape.
- Technology briefings and updates are a standing component of Board and committee meeting agendas, not discretionary enrichment activities.
- The BNRC monitors competency currency as an ongoing responsibility, not only at the time of formal annual assessments.

**Step 1 – Self-Assessment Score**

Each Board member's self-assessment scores are compiled by the Company Secretary and tabulated against the minimum requirements in Section 6.

**Step 2 – Facilitator Assessment Score**

The facilitator provides an independent assessment of each Board member's proficiency level based on the facilitator-led assessment session. Where the facilitator's assessment differs materially from the self-assessment, a reconciliation discussion is held with the Board member.

**Step 3 – Validated Score**

The BNRC reviews the self-assessment and facilitator scores and, with reference to continuous observation records, determines a validated proficiency score for each Board member across each domain.

**Step 4 – Gap Identification**

Validated scores are compared against minimum requirements. Any score below the minimum required level for the relevant role constitutes a **competency gap** requiring development action.

**Step 5 – Collective Competency Assessment**

The BNRC assesses the Board's collective competency profile against the collective requirements in Section 6.3 and determines whether collective gaps exist.

**Step 6 – BNRC Determination**

The BNRC formally determines whether:

- Individual Board members meet their minimum proficiency requirements (Pass / Gap / Not Assessed)
- The Board collectively meets collective competency requirements (Adequate / Collective Gap)

**Step 7 – Board Reporting**

Results are reported to the full Board and recorded in the Board minutes.
